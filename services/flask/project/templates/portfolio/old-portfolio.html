Project A

A funcar made with passion by enthousiasts

0%

In the first year of Automotive engineering we had to design and build a Funcar, within given restrictions. These restrictions included given measurements and a maximum power, braking power and speed. We were free to choose our concept but were only given a €500 budget, this meant we had to find (sponsored) parts to build the final car.

I mostly focussed on the dashboard behind the steering wheel. It used a webpage displayed on full-screen mode to present the speed, RPM of the engine, current gear and cooling water temperature of the car. All of this ran on a Raspberry Pi.
The information displayed, was retrieved by a set of Arduinos. A HAL sensor connected near the front wheels measured the speed of the car and another HAL sensor next to an outgoing engine shaft measured the engine’s RPM. The appropriate Arduinos then send their collected data to the Raspberry Pi via the I2C bus.
As the Honda CX500 motorcycle had a sequential gearbox, it was possible to simply count up and down to keep track in what gear the car is. This was done by fitting 2 push buttons near the shifting stick - one in front of it and one behind it.

Unfortunately the evening before the test day, our display broke because it had to much tension when assembled in it's frame. Nevertheless, we all had a lot of fun making this car, and we are proud to say that we won the price for best car of all project groups!

  • Date: October 2017 - July 2018
  • Client: Hogeschool Rotterdam
  • Category: Automotive engineering

"Torro Rosso"

An oldschool lawnmower, with a modern touch

For this project, we were assigned to buy a seated lawnmower in groups of 5 to 8 students. Our task was to improve engine power, fuel economy and emmision reduction. We started with a baseline measurement done with a Dynometer, in order to have a reference point and to find out more about what state our engine was in at that moment.

We were allowed to change the intake manifold and exhaust system and received an ECU package from "van Kronenburg" including all needed sensors, as sponsoring. On the basis of computational airflow simulations, we then designed a new intake and exhaust system. After this, we started fitting all the sensor in place, so we could test these in an early stage. We then mapped our new ECU with a correct mixture of fuel and air and correct pre-ignition values.

Unfortunately we did not pay enough attention to our drive line and were not able to retrieve any comparable data after the second run on the Dyno. It was a really diverse project in that we learned how to make our own wire harness, how to design and substantiate intake manifolds and exhaust systems and how to map an ECU correctly.

  • Date: September 2018 - Februari 2019
  • Client: Hogeschool Rotterdam
  • Category: Automotive engineering

Step sequencer

I have been working for this project on and off for a few weeks in 2018. The goal of this project is to make a step sequencer that can send MIDI commands to other MIDI devices, ideally, via USB. In that way it can be hooked up as if it were a keyboard. This design requires 16 rotary encoders, each encoder resembling a note. Every encoder can be used to change the tone of that note. You can also click the encoder to disable or enable a note. Each encoder also has 3 LEDs inside, which are to be used as an indicator of which note is playing.

After designing the first prototype on a small breadboard and making that into a CAD PCB, the PCB design could be sent off to China. After receiving 5 green boards, I soldered all needed components in place on one of the five boards. However, the design was converted from a small prototype to an 8 times as large PCB, this meant things were prone to go wrong. The so called "Shift register" integrated chips that were used, started to act weird and the board did not work nearly as expected.

Reflecting on this first prototype, it became evident the used Arduinos could only support around 10-15mA per output pin, which was greatly exceeded in some cases. On top of this, a few extra components should be placed in order to filter out any noise in the data bus of the shift registers. Lastly, all transistors in the left lower corner could be made redundant. In the near future I will elaborate on these ideas to create a second prototype.

  • Client: LinuXcien B.V.
  • Category: Electronic engineering

The Lost Colors

A mobile 2D rouguelike adventure game

The Lost Colors is a 2D mobile adventure game that I worked on during my minor "Game Design and Development" during my Automotive study at the Hogeschool Rotterdam. It was made as a team of 5 students. My task in this project was to realise the designed mechanics and game elements in the Unity game engine. I mostly worked on programming all the core game mechanics like the health system, simple enemy AI and combat system. Together with one other programmer, we managed to get all of the game elements working (relatively bug-free) within six weeks.


The story of the game is as follows:
In the world of The Lost Colors there is great chaos. Villages are continuously attacked and looted by monsters that leave nothing behind. In fact, these monsters became so greedy that at one point all villages were completely looted. The greed-driven monsters just couldn't settle for the loot they acquired and found a way to raid the colors from the world. When the colors had disappeared from the world, the world slowly came to a standstill and eventually came to a silence. Our hero was a humble knight and he, along with his friends (a merchant & a blacksmith) were tired of living in all this chaos. So they fled into the mountains to secretly set up a village where they could live in peace. However, as the world lost more and more color, the monsters also ended up in the mountain range. When the hero steps outside one day, almost the entire village has lost its color. Now is the time when the hero must take his sword and defeat the monsters once and for all.

I thoroughly enjoyed working on this project, since I learned a lot of new programming skills within these six weeks. Next to learning new skills, I am also really glad that we had to release the game on a official platform. This made sure we focussed on releasing a complete game. The game has been released for Android on the Play Store and can be found here.

Digital Motorsport Display

A digital CAN display for rally or circuit drivers

For my graduation internship, I found an assignment at van Kronenburg Motormanagement Systems in Geldrop, Netherlands. They had a working prototype of a digital motorsport display. This product was in a really early stage of development, when I started my assignment. The goal of the assignment was to make the product ready for production. To achieve this goal, I had to work on a lot of different subjects, in quite a few different disciplines.


    Some of the product specifications are as follows:
    - The user is able to display CAN data from their ECU on a 'Check Engine Page' and an 'Race Page'.
    - The product has 3 buttons to navigate through the menu (3 more are delivered to mount in the vehicle).
    - There are 16 configurable LEDs at the sides of the screen (2 sets of 8), so the user can receive warnings when engine data crosses a configured threshold
    - There are 16 RPM LEDs which turn on/off when a certain engine RPM has been passed, to show the user when to switch gears.
    - The product contains a GPS module, to be used with a lap-timer functionality. The end user is able to time how fast he has driven over any track in the EU!
    - The product contains an ambient light sensor, to detect the brightness of the ambient lighting, and adjusts LED and screen brightness accordingly.

The product is based on a Raspberry Pi Compute Module 4 as the main processor. This full-blown computer has been chosen, since the prototype was already built using a Raspberry Pi, and we wanted to make sure the hardware would be capable for at least 5 years. A custom 'carrier' printed circuit board has been designed (by and external company, under my supervision) to connect all the different sensors and actuators to each other and the Raspberry Pi.

The encasing of the product was also designed by me. Colleagues helped out making the product CNC routable. The screen is a 800x400px display, with 1000 nits of brightness. This screen has been specially sourced to make sure it is clearly readable in bright conditions, without glaring. The end product has a glass screen protector over top, to make sure that the screen itself does not get cracked easily.

The creation of this product has been a special experience. During this project, I have had a lot of responsibility on my shoulders, which was real unique for me. I have learned a lot about project management, complete product design, strong codebase design and codebase maintainability. Furthermore, I have learned a big deal about hardware designs and how embedded development is usually done. This project has given me a clearer vision of what I would like to do in the future of my career, creating products which combines hardware designs with some kind of software application.

  • Date: January 2021 - August 2022
  • Client: KMS
  • Category: Automotive engineering

Card Shuffler

An advanced card shuffler, capable of unfair play

Together with a good friend of mine, we designed an advanced card shuffler (albeit unnecessarily complex for it's current functionality). The idea of the machine is to (pseudo) randomly choose a number between 1 and 6. This random number is linked to one of six plastic 'trays' which can contain regular playing cards. The machine 'shoots' a card from the main stack into the randomly chosen tray. Once all cards from the stack have been shot, all cards are thrown on top of each other and the cycle can start again (or you consider the deck 'shuffled', and you can start playing your game).


The card shuffler is still in prototype phase. This prototype is made from off the shelf motors, linear rails, and driving belts. All the other components seen in the image are 3D printed and designed with CAD. The electronics schematic is also custom-made, and currently realised on a piece of hand soldered 'perf-board'. The brain of this device is a Teensy Microcontroller. This microcontroller was chosen because it packs a punch for it's small form factor. A lot of IO is also always pleasant for a device with this scale (3 stepper motors, 1 DC motor, 3 end-limiting switches, an RGB rotary encoder with push button and a small display).


A lot of parts have been 3D printed in this design. Even the (helical spiral) gears are 3D FDM printed! This allowed us to fine-tune the gearing exactly to our liking. Because of this fine-tuning ability, we were able to make the machine (almost) always dispense one card (not 0 and not more than 1 at a time). Because the product only dispenses one card at a time, it is possible to read which card is up next. Reading a card one at a time would mean that we could decide how to deck will look like beforehand to add a 'cheating mode'. The current prototype is not capable of this yet. We would need to add a camera and another computer to process the camera images correctly. We are currently not following up on this idea, since we would like to get the main functionality in a more complete and stable state first.

The next logical step is to change our hand soldered electronics into a PCB design. We also need to finish our encasing, and make a couple of changes to our mechanical setup to make sure only one card is dispensed. After that, we need to optimize speed and minimize noise made by the device, to make it's usability more practical. It currently shuffles one deck of cards in +/- 50 seconds (which means you need to play with another deck while the machine shuffles, in the current prototype stage).

Since this project started as a hobby, we have not done any kind of market research to know if it would be viable to mass produce and sell the product (if appropriate adjustments have been made to the design). We might look into this in the future, but first we want to finish the next prototype (just for fun).
